<p><i><b>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</b></i> (commonly shortened to <i><b>Alice in Wonderland</b></i>) is an 1865 <a href="/wiki/English_novel" title="English novel">novel</a> written by English author Charles Lutwidge Dodgson under the pseudonym <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Lewis Carroll</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It tells of a girl named <a href="/wiki/Alice_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Alice (Alice&#39;s Adventures in Wonderland)">Alice</a> falling through a rabbit hole into a fantasy world populated by peculiar, <a href="/wiki/Anthropomorphism" title="Anthropomorphism">anthropomorphic</a> creatures. The tale plays with <a href="/wiki/Logic" title="Logic">logic</a>, giving the story lasting popularity with adults as well as with children.<sup id="cite_ref-Lecercle_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lecercle-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is considered to be one of the best examples of the <a href="/wiki/Literary_nonsense" title="Literary nonsense">literary nonsense</a> genre.<sup id="cite_ref-Lecercle_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lecercle-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Schwab_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schwab-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Its <a href="/wiki/Narrative" title="Narrative">narrative</a> course and structure, <a href="/wiki/Character_(arts)" title="Character (arts)">characters</a> and imagery have been enormously influential<sup id="cite_ref-Schwab_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schwab-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> in both popular culture and literature, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a> genre.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Alice%27s_Adventures_Under_Ground_-_Lewis_Carroll_-_British_Library_Add_MS_46700_f45v.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Alice%27s_Adventures_Under_Ground_-_Lewis_Carroll_-_British_Library_Add_MS_46700_f45v.jpg/220px-Alice%27s_Adventures_Under_Ground_-_Lewis_Carroll_-_British_Library_Add_MS_46700_f45v.jpg" width="220" height="344" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Alice%27s_Adventures_Under_Ground_-_Lewis_Carroll_-_British_Library_Add_MS_46700_f45v.jpg/330px-Alice%27s_Adventures_Under_Ground_-_Lewis_Carroll_-_British_Library_Add_MS_46700_f45v.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Alice%27s_Adventures_Under_Ground_-_Lewis_Carroll_-_British_Library_Add_MS_46700_f45v.jpg/440px-Alice%27s_Adventures_Under_Ground_-_Lewis_Carroll_-_British_Library_Add_MS_46700_f45v.jpg 2x" data-file-width="479" data-file-height="750" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alice%27s_Adventures_Under_Ground_-_Lewis_Carroll_-_British_Library_Add_MS_46700_f45v.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Page from the original manuscript copy of <i>Alice's Adventures Under Ground</i>, 1864</div></div></div>
<p><i>Alice</i> was published in 1865, three years after <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lutwidge_Dodgson" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Lutwidge Dodgson">Charles Lutwidge Dodgson</a> and the Reverend <a href="/wiki/Robinson_Duckworth" title="Robinson Duckworth">Robinson Duckworth</a> rowed in a boat, on 4 July 1862<sup id="cite_ref-Story_Museum_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Story_Museum-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> (this popular date of the "golden afternoon"<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> might be a confusion or even another Alice-tale, for that particular day was cool, cloudy and rainy<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>), up <a href="/wiki/The_Isis" title="The Isis">the Isis</a> with the three young daughters of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Liddell" title="Henry Liddell">Henry Liddell</a> (the Vice-Chancellor of Oxford University and Dean of Christ Church): Lorina Charlotte Liddell (aged 13, born 1849) ("Prima" in the book's prefatory verse); <a href="/wiki/Alice_Liddell" title="Alice Liddell">Alice Pleasance Liddell</a> (aged 10, born 1852) ("Secunda" in the prefatory verse); Edith Mary Liddell (aged 8, born 1853) ("Tertia" in the prefatory verse).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The journey began at <a href="/wiki/Folly_Bridge" title="Folly Bridge">Folly Bridge</a> near <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a> and ended five miles away in the village of <a href="/wiki/Godstow" title="Godstow">Godstow</a>. During the trip Charles Dodgson told the girls a story that featured a bored little girl named Alice who goes looking for an adventure. The girls loved it, and Alice Liddell asked Dodgson to write it down for her. He began writing the manuscript of the story the next day, although that earliest version no longer exists. The girls and Dodgson took another boat trip a month later when he elaborated the plot to the story of Alice, and in November he began working on the manuscript in earnest.<sup id="cite_ref-C57_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C57-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>To add the finishing touches he researched natural history for the animals presented in the book, and then had the book examined by other children—particularly the children of <a href="/wiki/George_MacDonald" title="George MacDonald">George MacDonald</a>. He added his own illustrations but approached <a href="/wiki/John_Tenniel" title="John Tenniel">John Tenniel</a> to illustrate the book for publication, telling him that the story had been well liked by children.<sup id="cite_ref-C57_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C57-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 26 November 1864 he gave Alice the handwritten manuscript of <i>Alice's Adventures Under Ground</i>, with illustrations by Dodgson himself, dedicating it as "A Christmas Gift to a Dear Child in Memory of a Summer's Day".<sup id="cite_ref-Ray_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ray-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Some, including <a href="/wiki/Martin_Gardner" title="Martin Gardner">Martin Gardner</a>, speculate there was an earlier version that was destroyed later by Dodgson when he wrote a more elaborate copy by hand.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>But before Alice received her copy, Dodgson was already preparing it for publication and expanding the 15,500-word original to 27,500 words,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> most notably adding the episodes about the Cheshire Cat and the Mad Tea-Party.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synopsis">Synopsis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Synopsis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Alice_par_John_Tenniel_02.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Alice_par_John_Tenniel_02.png/170px-Alice_par_John_Tenniel_02.png" width="170" height="260" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Alice_par_John_Tenniel_02.png/255px-Alice_par_John_Tenniel_02.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Alice_par_John_Tenniel_02.png/340px-Alice_par_John_Tenniel_02.png 2x" data-file-width="789" data-file-height="1207" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alice_par_John_Tenniel_02.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/White_Rabbit" title="White Rabbit">White Rabbit</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Chapter One – Down the Rabbit Hole</b>: <a href="/wiki/Alice_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Alice (Alice&#39;s Adventures in Wonderland)">Alice</a>, a girl of seven years, is feeling bored and drowsy while sitting on the riverbank with her elder sister. She then notices a talking, clothed <a href="/wiki/White_Rabbit" title="White Rabbit">White Rabbit</a> with a pocket watch run past. She follows it down a rabbit hole when suddenly she falls a long way to a curious hall with many locked doors of all sizes. She finds a small key to a door too small for her to fit through, but through it she sees an attractive garden. She then discovers a bottle on a table labelled "DRINK ME," the contents of which cause her to shrink too small to reach the key which she has left on the table. She eats a cake with "EAT ME" written on it in currants as the chapter closes.
</p><p><b>Chapter Two – The Pool of Tears</b>: Chapter Two opens with Alice growing to such a tremendous size her head hits the ceiling. Alice is unhappy and, as she cries, her tears flood the hallway. After shrinking down again due to a fan she had picked up, Alice swims through her own tears and meets a <a href="/wiki/Mouse_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Mouse (Alice&#39;s Adventures in Wonderland)">Mouse</a>, who is swimming as well. She tries to make small talk with him in elementary French (thinking he may be a French mouse) but her opening gambit "Où est ma chatte?" ("Where is my cat?") offends the mouse and he tries to escape her.
</p><p><b>Chapter Three – The Caucus Race and a Long Tale</b>: The sea of tears becomes crowded with other animals and birds that have been swept away by the rising waters. Alice and the other animals convene on the bank and the question among them is how to get dry again. The Mouse gives them a very dry lecture on <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Dodo_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Dodo (Alice&#39;s Adventures in Wonderland)">Dodo</a> decides that the best thing to dry them off would be a Caucus-Race, which consists of everyone running in a circle with no clear winner. Alice eventually frightens all the animals away, unwittingly, by talking about her (moderately ferocious) cat.
</p><p><b>Chapter Four – The Rabbit Sends a Little Bill</b>: The White Rabbit appears again in search of the Duchess's gloves and fan. Mistaking her for his maidservant, Mary Ann, he orders Alice to go into the house and retrieve them.  Inside the house she finds another little bottle and drinks from it; immediately she starts growing again. The horrified Rabbit orders his gardener, <a href="/wiki/Bill_the_Lizard" title="Bill the Lizard">Bill the Lizard</a>, to climb on the roof and go down the chimney. Outside, Alice hears the voices of animals that have gathered to gawk at her giant arm. The crowd hurls pebbles at her, which turn into little cakes. Alice eats them, and they reduce her again in size.
</p><p><b>Chapter Five – Advice from a Caterpillar</b>: Alice comes upon a mushroom and sitting on it is a blue <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Caterpillar (Alice&#39;s Adventures in Wonderland)">Caterpillar</a> smoking a <a href="/wiki/Hookah" title="Hookah">hookah</a>. The Caterpillar questions Alice and she admits to her current identity crisis, compounded by her inability to remember a poem. Before crawling away, the caterpillar tells Alice that one side of the mushroom will make her taller and the other side will make her shorter. She breaks off two pieces from the mushroom. One side makes her shrink smaller than ever, while another causes her neck to grow high into the trees, where a pigeon mistakes her for a serpent. With some effort, Alice brings herself back to her normal height. She stumbles upon a small estate and uses the mushroom to reach a more appropriate height.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:De_Alice%27s_Abenteuer_im_Wunderland_Carroll_pic_23_edited_1_of_2.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/De_Alice%27s_Abenteuer_im_Wunderland_Carroll_pic_23_edited_1_of_2.png/220px-De_Alice%27s_Abenteuer_im_Wunderland_Carroll_pic_23_edited_1_of_2.png" width="220" height="137" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/De_Alice%27s_Abenteuer_im_Wunderland_Carroll_pic_23_edited_1_of_2.png/330px-De_Alice%27s_Abenteuer_im_Wunderland_Carroll_pic_23_edited_1_of_2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/De_Alice%27s_Abenteuer_im_Wunderland_Carroll_pic_23_edited_1_of_2.png/440px-De_Alice%27s_Abenteuer_im_Wunderland_Carroll_pic_23_edited_1_of_2.png 2x" data-file-width="2648" data-file-height="1647" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:De_Alice%27s_Abenteuer_im_Wunderland_Carroll_pic_23_edited_1_of_2.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Cheshire_Cat" title="Cheshire Cat">Cheshire Cat</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Chapter Six – Pig and Pepper</b>: A Fish-Footman has an invitation for the <a href="/wiki/Duchess_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Duchess (Alice&#39;s Adventures in Wonderland)">Duchess</a> of the house, which he delivers to a Frog-Footman. Alice observes this transaction and, after a perplexing conversation with the frog, lets herself into the house. The Duchess's Cook is throwing dishes and making a soup that has too much pepper, which causes Alice, the Duchess, and her baby (but not the cook or grinning <a href="/wiki/Cheshire_Cat" title="Cheshire Cat">Cheshire Cat</a>) to sneeze violently. Alice is given the baby by the Duchess and to her surprise, the baby turns into a pig. The Cheshire Cat appears in a tree, directing her to the <a href="/wiki/March_Hare" title="March Hare">March Hare</a>'s house. He disappears but his grin remains behind to float on its own in the air prompting Alice to remark that she has often seen a cat without a grin but never a grin without a cat.
</p><p><b>Chapter Seven – A Mad Tea-Party</b>: Alice becomes a guest at a "mad" tea party along with the <a href="/wiki/March_Hare" title="March Hare">March Hare</a>, the <a href="/wiki/The_Hatter" class="mw-redirect" title="The Hatter">Hatter</a>, and a very tired <a href="/wiki/Dormouse_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dormouse (Alice&#39;s Adventures in Wonderland)">Dormouse</a> who falls asleep frequently, only to be violently woken up moments later by the March Hare and the Hatter. The characters give Alice many riddles and stories, including the famous '<a href="/wiki/Why_is_a_raven_like_a_writing_desk%3F" class="mw-redirect" title="Why is a raven like a writing desk?">Why is a raven like a writing desk?</a>'. The Hatter reveals that they have tea all day because Time has punished him by eternally standing still at 6&#160;pm (tea time). Alice becomes insulted and tired of being bombarded with riddles and she leaves claiming that it was the stupidest tea party that she had ever been to.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Alice_par_John_Tenniel_30.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Alice_par_John_Tenniel_30.png/170px-Alice_par_John_Tenniel_30.png" width="170" height="248" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Alice_par_John_Tenniel_30.png/255px-Alice_par_John_Tenniel_30.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Alice_par_John_Tenniel_30.png/340px-Alice_par_John_Tenniel_30.png 2x" data-file-width="2482" data-file-height="3614" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alice_par_John_Tenniel_30.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Alice trying to play <a href="/wiki/Croquet" title="Croquet">croquet</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Flamingo" title="Flamingo">Flamingo</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Chapter Eight – The Queen's Croquet Ground</b>: Alice leaves the tea party and enters the garden where she comes upon three living playing cards painting the white roses on a rose tree red because <a href="/wiki/Queen_of_Hearts_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Queen of Hearts (Alice&#39;s Adventures in Wonderland)">The Queen of Hearts</a> hates white roses. A procession of more cards, kings and queens and even the White Rabbit enters the garden. Alice then meets the King and Queen. The Queen, a figure difficult to please, introduces her trademark phrase "Off with his head!" which she utters at the slightest dissatisfaction with a subject. Alice is invited (or some might say ordered) to play a game of croquet with the Queen and the rest of her subjects but the game quickly descends into chaos. Live flamingos are used as mallets and hedgehogs as balls and Alice once again meets the Cheshire Cat. The Queen of Hearts then orders the Cat to be beheaded, only to have her executioner complain that this is impossible since the head is all that can be seen of him. Because the cat belongs to the Duchess, the Queen is prompted to release the Duchess from prison to resolve the matter.
</p><p><b>Chapter Nine – The Mock Turtle's Story</b>: The Duchess is brought to the croquet ground at Alice's request. She ruminates on finding morals in everything around her. The Queen of Hearts dismisses her on the threat of execution and she introduces Alice to the <a href="/wiki/Gryphon_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Gryphon (Alice&#39;s Adventures in Wonderland)">Gryphon</a>, who takes her to the <a href="/wiki/Mock_Turtle" title="Mock Turtle">Mock Turtle</a>. The Mock Turtle is very sad, even though he has no sorrow. He tries to tell his story about how he used to be a real turtle in school, which the Gryphon interrupts so they can play a game.
</p><p><b>Chapter Ten – Lobster Quadrille</b>: The Mock Turtle and the Gryphon dance to the Lobster Quadrille, while Alice recites (rather incorrectly) "<a href="/wiki/%27Tis_the_Voice_of_the_Lobster" title="&#39;Tis the Voice of the Lobster">'Tis the Voice of the Lobster</a>". The Mock Turtle sings them "Beautiful Soup" during which the Gryphon drags Alice away for an impending trial.
</p><p><b>Chapter Eleven – Who Stole the Tarts?</b>: Alice attends a trial whereby the <a href="/wiki/Knave_of_Hearts_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Knave of Hearts (Alice&#39;s Adventures in Wonderland)">Knave of Hearts</a> is accused of stealing the Queen's tarts. The jury is composed of various animals, including <a href="/wiki/Bill_the_Lizard" title="Bill the Lizard">Bill the Lizard</a>, the White Rabbit is the court's trumpeter, and the judge is the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Hearts_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="King of Hearts (Alice&#39;s Adventures in Wonderland)">King of Hearts</a>.
During the proceedings, Alice finds that she is steadily growing larger. The dormouse scolds Alice and tells her she has no right to grow at such a rapid pace and take up all the air. Alice scoffs and calls the dormouse's accusation ridiculous because everyone grows and she cannot help it. Meanwhile, witnesses at the trial include the Hatter, who displeases and frustrates the King through his indirect answers to the questioning, and the Duchess's cook.
</p><p><b>Chapter Twelve – Alice's Evidence</b>: Alice is then called up as a witness. She accidentally knocks over the jury box with the animals inside them and the King orders the animals be placed back into their seats before the trial continues. The King and Queen order Alice to be gone, citing Rule 42 ("All persons more than a mile high to leave the court"), but Alice disputes their judgement and refuses to leave. She argues with the King and Queen of Hearts over the ridiculous proceedings, eventually refusing to hold her tongue. The Queen shouts her familiar "Off with her head!" but Alice is unafraid, calling them out as just a pack of cards; just as they start to swarm over her. Alice's sister wakes her up from a dream, brushing what turns out to be some leaves and not a shower of playing cards from Alice's face. Alice leaves her sister on the bank to imagine all the curious happenings for herself.
</p>

(1923)</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland_-_Carroll,_Robinson_-_S119_-_%27What_day_of_the_month_is_it%27_he_said,_turning_to_Alice.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland_-_Carroll%2C_Robinson_-_S119_-_%27What_day_of_the_month_is_it%27_he_said%2C_turning_to_Alice.jpg/220px-Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland_-_Carroll%2C_Robinson_-_S119_-_%27What_day_of_the_month_is_it%27_he_said%2C_turning_to_Alice.jpg" width="220" height="296" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland_-_Carroll%2C_Robinson_-_S119_-_%27What_day_of_the_month_is_it%27_he_said%2C_turning_to_Alice.jpg/330px-Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland_-_Carroll%2C_Robinson_-_S119_-_%27What_day_of_the_month_is_it%27_he_said%2C_turning_to_Alice.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland_-_Carroll%2C_Robinson_-_S119_-_%27What_day_of_the_month_is_it%27_he_said%2C_turning_to_Alice.jpg/440px-Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland_-_Carroll%2C_Robinson_-_S119_-_%27What_day_of_the_month_is_it%27_he_said%2C_turning_to_Alice.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1616" data-file-height="2177" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland_-_Carroll,_Robinson_-_S119_-_%27What_day_of_the_month_is_it%27_he_said,_turning_to_Alice.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mad tea party. <a href="/wiki/Theophilus_Carter" title="Theophilus Carter">Theophilus Carter</a> has been suggested as a model for The Hatter</div></div></div>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Annotated_Alice" title="The Annotated Alice">The Annotated Alice</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Gardner" title="Martin Gardner">Martin Gardner</a> provides background information for the characters. The members of the boating party that first heard <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Carroll</a>'s tale show up in Chapter 3 ("A Caucus-Race and a Long Tale"). <a href="/wiki/Alice_Liddell" title="Alice Liddell">Alice Liddell</a> herself is there, while Carroll is caricatured as the Dodo (because Dodgson stuttered when he spoke, he sometimes pronounced his last name as <i>Dodo-Dodgson</i>). The Duck refers to <a href="/wiki/Robinson_Duckworth" title="Robinson Duckworth">Canon Duckworth</a>, and the Lory and Eaglet to Alice Liddell's sisters Lorina and Edith.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Bill the Lizard may be a play on the name of British Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Disraeli" title="Benjamin Disraeli">Benjamin Disraeli</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> One of Tenniel's illustrations in <i><a href="/wiki/Through_the_Looking-Glass" title="Through the Looking-Glass">Through the Looking-Glass</a></i> — the 1871 sequel to <i>Alice</i> — depicts the character referred to as the "Man in White Paper" (whom Alice meets as a fellow passenger riding on the train with her) as a caricature of Disraeli, wearing a paper hat.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The illustrations of the Lion and the Unicorn (also in <i>Looking-Glass</i>) also bear a striking resemblance to Tenniel's <i><a href="/wiki/Punch_(magazine)" title="Punch (magazine)">Punch</a></i> illustrations of <a href="/wiki/William_Ewart_Gladstone" title="William Ewart Gladstone">Gladstone</a> and Disraeli.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It has been suggested by some writers that The Hatter is a reference to <a href="/wiki/Theophilus_Carter" title="Theophilus Carter">Theophilus Carter</a>, a furniture dealer known in <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>. Tenniel apparently drew the Hatter to resemble Carter, on a suggestion of Carroll's.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> However, it is unlikely that Carter was the model for The Hatter, and there is no evidence that Carroll ever invited Tenniel to Oxford for any purpose.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The Dormouse tells a story about three little sisters named Elsie, Lacie, and Tillie. These are the Liddell sisters: Elsie is L.C. (Lorina Charlotte), Tillie is Edith (her family nickname is Matilda), and Lacie is an <a href="/wiki/Anagram" title="Anagram">anagram</a> of Alice.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Mock Turtle speaks of a Drawling-master, "an old conger eel", who came once a week to teach "Drawling, Stretching, and Fainting in Coils". This is a reference to the art critic <a href="/wiki/John_Ruskin" title="John Ruskin">John Ruskin</a>, who came once a week to the Liddell house to teach the children <i>drawing</i>, <i>sketching</i>, and <i>painting in oils</i>. (The children did, in fact, learn well; Alice Liddell, for one, produced a number of skilful watercolours.)<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Mock Turtle also sings "Turtle Soup". This is a parody of a song called "Star of the Evening, Beautiful Star", which was performed as a trio by Lorina, Alice and Edith Liddell for Lewis Carroll in the Liddell home during the same summer in which he first told the story of <i>Alice's Adventures Under Ground</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Poems_and_songs">Poems and songs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Poems and songs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carroll wrote multiple poems and songs for <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i>, including:
</p>
<ul><li>"<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/All_in_the_Golden_Afternoon" class="extiw" title="s:All in the Golden Afternoon">All in the golden afternoon...</a>"—the prefatory verse, an original poem by Carroll that recalls the rowing expedition on which he first told the story of Alice's adventures underground</li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/How_Doth_the_Little_Crocodile" title="How Doth the Little Crocodile">How Doth the Little Crocodile</a>"—a parody of <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Watts" title="Isaac Watts">Isaac Watts</a>' nursery rhyme, "<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Against_Idleness_and_Mischief" class="extiw" title="wikisource:Against Idleness and Mischief">Against Idleness and Mischief</a>"</li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/The_Mouse%27s_Tale" title="The Mouse&#39;s Tale">The Mouse's Tale</a>"—an example of <a href="/wiki/Concrete_poetry" title="Concrete poetry">concrete poetry</a></li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/You_Are_Old,_Father_William" title="You Are Old, Father William">You Are Old, Father William</a>"—a parody of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Southey" title="Robert Southey">Robert Southey</a>'s "<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Old_Man%27s_Comforts_and_How_He_Gained_Them" class="extiw" title="wikisource:The Old Man&#39;s Comforts and How He Gained Them">The Old Man's Comforts and How He Gained Them</a>"</li>
<li>The Duchess's lullaby, "Speak roughly to your little boy..."—a parody of <a href="/wiki/David_Bates_(poet)" title="David Bates (poet)">David Bates</a>' "Speak Gently"</li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/Twinkle,_Twinkle,_Little_Bat" title="Twinkle, Twinkle, Little Bat">Twinkle, Twinkle, Little Bat</a>"—a parody of <a href="/wiki/Jane_Taylor_(poet)" title="Jane Taylor (poet)">Jane Taylor</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Twinkle_Twinkle_Little_Star" class="mw-redirect" title="Twinkle Twinkle Little Star">Twinkle Twinkle Little Star</a>"</li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/The_Lobster_Quadrille" class="mw-redirect" title="The Lobster Quadrille">The Lobster Quadrille</a>"—a parody of <a href="/wiki/Mary_Botham_Howitt" class="mw-redirect" title="Mary Botham Howitt">Mary Botham Howitt</a>'s "<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Spider_and_the_Fly" class="extiw" title="wikisource:The Spider and the Fly">The Spider and the Fly</a>"</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_style_and_themes">Writing style and themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Writing style and themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Symbolism">Symbolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Symbolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Martin_Gardner" title="Martin Gardner">Martin Gardner</a> and other scholars have shown the book to be filled with many parodies of Victorian popular culture, suggesting it belongs in spirit with <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_%26_Sullivan" class="mw-redirect" title="Gilbert &amp; Sullivan">Gilbert &amp; Sullivan</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Topsyturveydom" title="Topsyturveydom">Topsyturveydom</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most of the book's adventures may have been based on and influenced by people, situations and buildings in Oxford and at Christ Church, <i>e.g.</i>, the "Rabbit Hole," which symbolised the actual stairs in the back of the main hall in Christ Church. A carving of a griffon and rabbit, as seen in <a href="/wiki/Ripon_Cathedral" title="Ripon Cathedral">Ripon Cathedral</a>, where Carroll's father was a canon, may have provided inspiration for the tale.<sup id="cite_ref-Hello_Yorkshire_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hello_Yorkshire-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since Carroll was a mathematician at <a href="/wiki/Christ_Church,_Oxford" title="Christ Church, Oxford">Christ Church</a>, it has been suggested<sup id="cite_ref-more_annotated_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-more_annotated-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> that there are many references and mathematical concepts in both this story and <i><a href="/wiki/Through_the_Looking-Glass" title="Through the Looking-Glass">Through the Looking-Glass</a></i>; examples include:
</p>
<ul><li>In chapter 1, "Down the Rabbit-Hole", in the midst of shrinking, Alice waxes philosophic concerning what final size she will end up as, perhaps "<i>going out altogether, like a candle</i>"; this pondering reflects the concept of a <a href="/wiki/Limit_of_a_function" title="Limit of a function">limit</a>.</li>
<li>In chapter 2, "The Pool of Tears", Alice tries to perform multiplication but produces some odd results: "<i>Let me see: four times five is twelve, and four times six is thirteen, and four times seven is—oh dear! I shall never get to twenty at that rate!</i>" This explores the representation of numbers using different <a href="/wiki/Base_(exponentiation)" title="Base (exponentiation)">bases</a> and <a href="/wiki/Positional_notation" title="Positional notation">positional</a> <a href="/wiki/Numeral_system" title="Numeral system">numeral systems</a>: 4 × 5 = 12 in base 18 notation, 4 × 6 = 13 in base 21 notation, and 4 × 7 could be 14 in base 24 notation. Continuing this sequence, going up three bases each time, the result will continue to be less than 20 in the corresponding base notation. (After 4 × 12 = 19 in Base 39, the product would be 4 × 13 = 1A in Base 42, then 1B, 1C, 1D, and so on.)</li>
<li>In chapter 7, "A Mad Tea-Party", the March Hare, the Hatter, and the Dormouse give several examples in which the semantic value of a sentence <b>A</b> is not the same value of the <a href="/wiki/Antimetabole" title="Antimetabole">converse</a> of <b>A</b> (for example, "<i>Why, you might just as well say that 'I see what I eat' is the same thing as 'I eat what I see'!</i>"); in logic and mathematics, this is discussing a <a href="/wiki/Converse_relation" title="Converse relation">converse relation</a>.</li>
<li>Also in chapter 7, Alice ponders what it means when the changing of seats around the circular table places them back at the beginning. This is an observation of addition on the <a href="/wiki/Ring_of_integers" title="Ring of integers">ring of integers</a> <a href="/wiki/Modular_arithmetic" title="Modular arithmetic">modulo</a> N.</li>
<li>The Cheshire cat fades until it disappears entirely, leaving only its wide grin, suspended in the air, leading Alice to marvel and note that she has seen a cat without a grin, but never a grin without a cat. Deep abstraction of concepts, such as non-Euclidean geometry, abstract algebra, and the beginnings of mathematical logic, was taking over mathematics at the time Dodgson was writing. Dodgson's delineation of the relationship between cat and grin can be taken to represent the very concept of mathematics and number itself. For example, instead of considering two or three apples, one may easily consider the concept of 'apple', upon which the concepts of 'two' and 'three' may seem to depend. A far more sophisticated jump is to consider the concepts of 'two' and 'three' by themselves, just like a grin, originally seemingly dependent on the cat, separated conceptually from its physical object.</li></ul>
<p>Literary scholar Melanie Bayley asserted in the magazine <i><a href="/wiki/New_Scientist" title="New Scientist">New Scientist</a></i> that Dodgson wrote <i>Alice in Wonderland</i> in its final form as a scathing satire on new modern mathematics that were emerging in the mid-19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It has been suggested by several people, including <a href="/wiki/Martin_Gardner" title="Martin Gardner">Martin Gardner</a> and Selwyn Goodacre,<sup id="cite_ref-more_annotated_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-more_annotated-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> that Dodgson had an interest in the French language, choosing to make references and puns about it in the story. It is most likely that these are references to French lessons—a common feature of a Victorian middle-class girl's upbringing. For example, in the second chapter Alice posits that the mouse may be French. She therefore chooses to speak the first sentence of her French lesson-book to it: "<i>Où est ma chatte?</i>" ("Where is my cat?"). In Henri Bué's French translation, Alice posits that the mouse may be Italian and speaks Italian to it.
</p><p>Pat's "Digging for apples" could be a <a href="/wiki/Cross-language_pun" class="mw-redirect" title="Cross-language pun">cross-language pun</a>, as <i>pomme de terre</i> (literally; "apple of the earth") means potato and <i>pomme</i> means apple.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the second chapter, Alice initially addresses the mouse as "O Mouse", based on her memory of the noun <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">declensions</a> "in her brother's <a href="/wiki/Instruction_in_Latin#Order_of_cases" title="Instruction in Latin">Latin Grammar</a>, 'A mouse – of a mouse – to a mouse – a mouse – O mouse!'" These words correspond to the first five of Latin's six cases, in a traditional order established by medieval grammarians: <i>mus</i> (<a href="/wiki/Nominative" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominative">nominative</a>), <i>muris</i> (<a href="/wiki/Genitive" class="mw-redirect" title="Genitive">genitive</a>), <i>muri</i> (<a href="/wiki/Dative" class="mw-redirect" title="Dative">dative</a>), <i>murem</i> (<a href="/wiki/Accusative" class="mw-redirect" title="Accusative">accusative</a>), <i>(O) mus</i> (<a href="/wiki/Vocative" class="mw-redirect" title="Vocative">vocative</a>). The sixth case, <i>mure</i> (<a href="/wiki/Ablative" class="mw-redirect" title="Ablative">ablative</a>) is absent from Alice's recitation.
</p><p>In the eighth chapter, three cards are painting the roses on a rose tree red, because they had accidentally planted a white-rose tree that <a href="/wiki/Queen_of_Hearts_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Queen of Hearts (Alice&#39;s Adventures in Wonderland)">The Queen of Hearts</a> hates. Red roses symbolised the English <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lancaster" title="House of Lancaster">House of Lancaster</a>, while white roses were the symbol for their rival <a href="/wiki/House_of_York" title="House of York">House of York</a>. This scene is an allusion to the <a href="/wiki/Wars_of_the_Roses" title="Wars of the Roses">Wars of the Roses</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While the book has remained in print and continually inspires new adaptations, the cultural material that it references has become largely specialized knowledge. <a href="/wiki/Leon_Coward" title="Leon Coward">Dr Leon Coward</a> asserts the book 'suffers' from "readings which reflect today's fascination with postmodernism and psychology, rather than delving into an historically informed interpretation," and speculates that this has been partly driven by audiences encountering the narrative through a 'second-hand' source, explaining "our impressions of the original text are based on a multiplicity of reinterpretations. We don't necessarily realise we're missing anything in understanding the original product, because we're usually never dealing <i>with</i> the original product."<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eating_and_devouring">Eating and devouring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Eating and devouring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Carina Garland notes how the world is "expressed via representations of food and appetite", naming Alice's frequent desire for consumption (of both food and words), her 'Curious Appetites'.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Often, the idea of eating coincides to make gruesome images. After the riddle "Why is a raven like a writing-desk?", the Hatter claims that Alice might as well say, "I see what I eat…I eat what I see" and so the riddle’s solution, put forward by Boe Birns,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> could be that "A raven eats worms; a writing desk is worm-eaten"; this idea of food encapsulates idea of life feeding on life, for the worm is being eaten and then becomes the eater &#160;&#8211;&#32; a horrific image of mortality.
</p><p>Nina Auerbach discusses how the novel revolves around eating and drinking which "motivates much of her [Alice's] behaviour", for the story is essentially about things "entering and leaving her mouth"<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The animals of Wonderland are of particular interest, for Alice's relation to them shifts constantly because, as Lovell-Smith states, Alice's changes in size continually reposition her in the food chain, serving as a way to make her acutely aware of the ‘eat or be eaten’ attitude that permeates Wonderland.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Illustrations">Illustrations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Illustrations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:AAUG_p06.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/AAUG_p06.png/220px-AAUG_p06.png" width="220" height="245" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/AAUG_p06.png/330px-AAUG_p06.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/AAUG_p06.png/440px-AAUG_p06.png 2x" data-file-width="1068" data-file-height="1189" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:AAUG_p06.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>One of the author's own illustrations.</div></div></div>
<p>The manuscript was illustrated by Dodgson himself who added 37 illustrations—printed in a <a href="/wiki/Facsimile" title="Facsimile">facsimile</a> edition in 1887.<sup id="cite_ref-Ray_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ray-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> John Tenniel provided 42 <a href="/wiki/Wood_engraving" title="Wood engraving">wood engraved</a> illustrations for the published version of the book. The first print run was destroyed (or sold to America<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>) at Carroll's request because he was dissatisfied with the quality. The book was reprinted and published in 1866.<sup id="cite_ref-Ray_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ray-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/John_Tenniel" title="John Tenniel">John Tenniel</a>'s illustrations of Alice do not portray the real <a href="/wiki/Alice_Liddell" title="Alice Liddell">Alice Liddell</a>, who had dark hair and a short fringe.
<i>Alice</i> has provided a challenge for other illustrators, including those of 1907 by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Pears" title="Charles Pears">Charles Pears</a> and the full series of colour plates and line-drawings by <a href="/wiki/Harry_Rountree" title="Harry Rountree">Harry Rountree</a> published in the (inter-War) Children's Press (Glasgow) edition. Other significant illustrators include: <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Rackham" title="Arthur Rackham">Arthur Rackham</a> (1907), <a href="/wiki/Willy_Pogany" title="Willy Pogany">Willy Pogany</a> (1929), <a href="/wiki/Mervyn_Peake" title="Mervyn Peake">Mervyn Peake</a> (1946), <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Steadman" title="Ralph Steadman">Ralph Steadman</a> (1967), <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD" title="Salvador Dalí">Salvador Dalí</a> (1969), Graham Overden (1969), <a href="/wiki/Max_Ernst" title="Max Ernst">Max Ernst</a> (1970), <a href="/wiki/Peter_Blake_(artist)" title="Peter Blake (artist)">Peter Blake</a> (1970), <a href="/wiki/Tove_Jansson" title="Tove Jansson">Tove Jansson</a> (1977), <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Browne_(illustrator)" class="mw-redirect" title="Anthony Browne (illustrator)">Anthony Browne</a> (1988), <a href="/wiki/Helen_Oxenbury" title="Helen Oxenbury">Helen Oxenbury</a> (1999) and <a href="/wiki/Lisbeth_Zwerger" title="Lisbeth Zwerger">Lisbeth Zwerger</a> (1999).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception_by_reviewers">Reception by reviewers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reception by reviewers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The book <i>Alice in Wonderland</i> failed to be named in an 1888 poll of the most popular children's stories. Generally it received poor reviews with reviewers giving more credit to Tenniel's illustrations than to Carroll's story. At the release of <i>Through the Looking-Glass</i>, the first Alice tale gained in popularity and by the end of the 19th century <a href="/wiki/Sir_Walter_Besant" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Walter Besant">Sir Walter Besant</a> wrote that <i>Alice in Wonderland</i> "was a book of that extremely rare kind which will belong to all the generations to come until the language becomes obsolete".<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication_history">Publication history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Publication history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:AlicesAdventuresInWonderlandTitlePage.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/AlicesAdventuresInWonderlandTitlePage.jpg/200px-AlicesAdventuresInWonderlandTitlePage.jpg" width="200" height="308" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/AlicesAdventuresInWonderlandTitlePage.jpg/300px-AlicesAdventuresInWonderlandTitlePage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/af/AlicesAdventuresInWonderlandTitlePage.jpg 2x" data-file-width="325" data-file-height="500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:AlicesAdventuresInWonderlandTitlePage.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Title page of the original edition (1865)</div></div></div>
<p>In 1865, Dodgson's tale was published as <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i> by "<a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Lewis Carroll</a>" with illustrations by <a href="/wiki/John_Tenniel" title="John Tenniel">John Tenniel</a>. The first print run of 2,000 was held back because Tenniel objected to the print quality.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> A new edition, released in December of the same year, but carrying an 1866 date, was quickly printed. The text blocks of the original edition were removed from the binding and sold with Dodgson's permission to the New York publishing house of <a href="/wiki/D._Appleton_%26_Company" title="D. Appleton &amp; Company">D. Appleton &amp; Company</a>. The binding for the Appleton <i>Alice</i> was virtually identical to the 1866 Macmillan <i>Alice</i>, except for the publisher's name at the foot of the spine. The title page of the Appleton <i>Alice</i> was an insert cancelling the original Macmillan title page of 1865, and bearing the New York publisher's imprint and the date 1866.
</p><p>The entire print run sold out quickly. <i>Alice</i> was a publishing sensation, beloved by children and adults alike. Among its first avid readers were <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> and the young <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Wilde" title="Oscar Wilde">Oscar Wilde</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The book has never been out of print. <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i> has been translated into at least 97 languages,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> or as many as 174 languages.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> There have now been over a hundred editions of the book, as well as countless adaptations in other media, especially theatre and film.
</p><p>The book is commonly referred to by the abbreviated title <i>Alice in Wonderland</i>, which has been popularised by the numerous stage, film and television adaptations of the story produced over the years. Some printings of this title contain both <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i> and its sequel <i><a href="/wiki/Through_the_Looking-Glass" title="Through the Looking-Glass">Through the Looking-Glass, and What Alice Found There</a></i>.
</p>

<p>The following list is a timeline of major publication events related to <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i>:
</p>
<ul><li>1865: First UK edition (the second printing).</li>
<li>1865: First US edition (the first printing of above).<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>1869: <i>Alice's Abenteuer im Wunderland</i><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> is published in German translation by Antonie Zimmermann.</li>
<li>1869: <i>Aventures d'Alice au pays des merveilles</i><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> is published in French translation by Henri Bué.</li>
<li>1870: <i>Alice's Äventyr i Sagolandet</i><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> is published in Swedish translation by Emily Nonnen.</li>
<li>1871: Dodgson meets another Alice during his time in London, Alice Raikes, and talks with her about her reflection in a mirror, leading to another book, <i><a href="/wiki/Through_the_Looking-Glass" title="Through the Looking-Glass">Through the Looking-Glass, and What Alice Found There</a></i>, which sells even better.</li>
<li>1872: <i>Le Avventure di Alice nel Paese delle Meraviglie</i><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> is published in Italian translation by Teodorico Pietrocòla Rossetti.</li>
<li>1879: Соня в царстве Дива. First Russian edition of "Lewis Carroll's Alice's Adventures in Wonderland" – in Russian.</li>
<li>1882: Selchow &amp; Righter publishes <i><a href="/wiki/The_Game_of_Alice_in_Wonderland" title="The Game of Alice in Wonderland">The Game of Alice in Wonderland</a></i>, the first game based on the book.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>1886: Carroll publishes a facsimile of the earlier <i>Alice's Adventures Under Ground</i> manuscript.</li>
<li>1890: Carroll publishes <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nursery_%22Alice%22" title="The Nursery &quot;Alice&quot;">The Nursery "Alice"</a></i>, a special edition "to be read by Children aged from Nought to Five".</li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Live_performance">Live performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Live performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first full major production of 'Alice' books during Carroll's lifetime was <i><a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Wonderland_(musical)" title="Alice in Wonderland (musical)">Alice in Wonderland</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Musical_theatre" title="Musical theatre">musical play</a> by H. Saville Clark (book) and <a href="/wiki/Walter_Slaughter" title="Walter Slaughter">Walter Slaughter</a> (music), which played in 1886 at the <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Wales_Theatre" title="Prince of Wales Theatre">Prince of Wales Theatre</a> in London.
</p><p>As the book and its sequel are Carroll's most widely recognised works, they have also inspired numerous live performances, including plays, operas, ballets, and traditional English <a href="/wiki/Pantomime" title="Pantomime">pantomimes</a>. These works range from fairly faithful adaptations to those that use the story as a basis for new works. An example of the latter is <i>The Eighth Square</i>, a murder mystery set in Wonderland, written by <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Fleming" title="Matthew Fleming">Matthew Fleming</a> and music and lyrics by Ben J. Macpherson. This goth-toned rock musical premiered in 2006 at the <a href="/wiki/New_Theatre_Royal" title="New Theatre Royal">New Theatre Royal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth" title="Portsmouth">Portsmouth</a>, England. The TA Fantastika, a popular <a href="/wiki/Black_light_theatre" title="Black light theatre">Black light theatre</a> in Prague performs "Aspects of Alice"; written and directed by Petr Kratochvíl. This adaptation is not faithful to the books, but rather explores Alice's journey into adulthood while incorporating allusions to the history of Czech Republic.
</p><p>Over the years, many notable people in the performing arts have been involved in <i>Alice</i> productions. Actress <a href="/wiki/Eva_Le_Gallienne" title="Eva Le Gallienne">Eva Le Gallienne</a> famously adapted both Alice books for the stage in 1932; this production has been revived in New York in 1947 and 1982. One of the most well-known American productions was <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Papp" title="Joseph Papp">Joseph Papp</a>'s 1980 staging of <i>Alice in Concert</i> at the <a href="/wiki/Public_Theater" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Theater">Public Theater</a> in New York City. <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Swados" title="Elizabeth Swados">Elizabeth Swados</a> wrote the book, lyrics, and music. Based on both <i>Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</i> and <i>Through the Looking-Glass</i>, Papp and Swados had previously produced a version of it at the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Shakespeare_Festival" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Shakespeare Festival">New York Shakespeare Festival</a>. <a href="/wiki/Meryl_Streep" title="Meryl Streep">Meryl Streep</a> played Alice, the White Queen, and Humpty Dumpty. The cast also included <a href="/wiki/Debbie_Allen" title="Debbie Allen">Debbie Allen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jeter" title="Michael Jeter">Michael Jeter</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mark_Linn-Baker" title="Mark Linn-Baker">Mark Linn-Baker</a>. Performed on a bare stage with the actors in modern dress, the play is a loose adaptation, with song styles ranging the globe. A community theater production of Alice was <a href="/wiki/Olivia_de_Havilland" title="Olivia de Havilland">Olivia de Havilland</a>'s first foray onto the stage.
</p><p>Similarly, the 1992 operatic production <i>Alice</i> used both <i>Alice</i> books as its inspiration. It also employs scenes with Charles Dodgson, a young Alice Liddell, and an adult Alice Liddell, to frame the story. Paul Schmidt wrote the play, with <a href="/wiki/Tom_Waits" title="Tom Waits">Tom Waits</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kathleen_Brennan" title="Kathleen Brennan">Kathleen Brennan</a> writing the music. Although the original production in <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, Germany, received only a small audience, Tom Waits released the songs as the album <i><a href="/wiki/Alice_(Tom_Waits_album)" title="Alice (Tom Waits album)">Alice</a></i> in 2002.
</p><p>A musical adaption was written by Michael Sirotta and Heather M. Dominick in 1997, titled <i>Alice in Wonderland, a Musical Adventure</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A ballet by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Wheeldon" title="Christopher Wheeldon">Christopher Wheeldon</a><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Wright_(playwright)" title="Nicholas Wright (playwright)">Nicholas Wright</a><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> commissioned for <a href="/wiki/The_Royal_Ballet" title="The Royal Ballet">The Royal Ballet</a> entitled "Alice's Adventures in Wonderland" premiered in February 2011 at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Opera_House" title="Royal Opera House">Royal Opera House</a> in Covent Garden, London. The ballet was based on the novel Wheeldon grew up reading as a child and is generally faithful to the original story, although some critics claimed it may have been too faithful.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> The ballet overall stays generally light hearted for its running time of an hour and forty minutes. The ballet returned to the Royal Opera House in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Works_influenced">Works influenced</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Works influenced">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Works_based_on_Alice_in_Wonderland" title="Works based on Alice in Wonderland">Works based on<i> Alice in Wonderland</i></a></div>
<p>Alice and the rest of Wonderland continue to inspire or influence many other works of art to this day, sometimes indirectly via the 1951 <a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Wonderland_(1951_film)" title="Alice in Wonderland (1951 film)">Disney movie</a>, for example. References, homages, reworkings and derivative works can be found in many works of literature, film, theatre, visual art, music, and games. The character of the plucky, yet proper, Alice has proven immensely popular and inspired similar heroines in literature and pop culture, many also named Alice in homage. 
</p>
